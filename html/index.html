I’m a student who was assigned to make a website about SPACEX. The things I discovered blew my mind:

Space privatization, Global commons, Starlink, ITU, competition and conflict.. a new space race era is ongoing in 2020.

The opinions expressed are my own. Share yours after reading.

Most of us have heard about SPACEX and the genius of Elon Musk.

Space buffs have heard about Starlink, SPACEX’s groundbreaking satellite project.

But few are aware that their satellite spree has finally forced the long delayed but inevitable conflict upon the us:

Should space be:

The shared property of humankind or The property of pioneering spacefaring nations and private enterprises?

Ever since the SPUTNIK (the first satellite in space) was launched in 1957, humans have (as of 8th. September 2020) put 8.370 satellites into space, of which 2.218 are operational.

1957 Sputnik

2020 SPACEX satellite

Of the 2218 operational satellites, 715 belong to SPACEX.

That equates to one third of every operational satellite in space in 2020

For a single company, that's impressive.

Actually, SPACEX has approval to launch 12.000 satellites into orbit. Now that's really ambitious.

Hold on - SPACEX has a pending application to raise that limit to 42.000. Whaa....

...what’s up with satellites these days?

This is what's up

The greatest space race since the launch of Sputnik in 1957 is taking place right now.

The most expansive colonisation events since the 19th century are lined up at our doorstep.

The race for "Low earth orbits" is on and SPACEX is in the lead.

SPACEX’s unprecedented satellite spree is due to their project, Starlink.

So, what is Starlink. and why does it require thousands of satellites?

The Good:

“...Starlink will deliver high speed broadband internet to locations where access has been unreliable, expensive, or completely unavailable.”

- Starlink.com

Basically, it's the greatest internet satellite service ever created. Despite massive technical hurdles yet to be overcome, Elon Musk’s track record of making the seemingly impossible, possible, does provide reason to be hopeful.

Half the world is without internet

and the benefits of internet coverage goes without saying. Currently, batches of 60 Starlink satellites are launched into orbit monthly, and internet latency tests are already happening. Things are looking promising.

The not so Good:

While space is vast, satellite orbits are not. Especially Low earth orbits (LEOs).

why?

Because their orbits are the closest to earth. Orbits further out than the LEOs result in dismal internet connection speeds, shortened satellite longevity, and increased space debris hazards.

Claiming and colonising space

is not about claiming sovereignity over empty cubic miles of the endless vacuum.

It is about claiming as many LEOs as possible.

Colonisation of the Moon is the next substance in line, and is likely to happen in 2024.

Could SPACEX exploit their brilliance and monopolize the remaining LEOs?

they could...

but they haven't...

because of...

or, more specifically, the ITU: an agency of the UN responsible for all matters related to information and communication technology.

ITU

There are two regulatory steps between SPACEX and putting another LEO in orbit:

1. Get approval by the American government agency FCC.

2. The FCC then applies to the ITU on behalf of SpaceX.

Current Status:

12.000 LEOs

Approved and ready for deploymemt

+30.000 LEOs

fcc: approved itu: pending (since oct. 2019)

what happens if......

itu approves 42.000 leos

A huge part of the world without internet might finally get it, yet this will likely piss off the international community because of SpaceX’s monopoly of highly competitive orbits.

itu rejects 42.000 leos

I suspect this scenario will reignite a highly discordant but inevitable argument: "the common heritage of humankind” principle and its relation to the substances of space.

global commons

"The common heritage of humankind" (also called “global commons”) is an an international law principle that establishes that some areas are to be "protected from exploitation by individual nation states or corporations."

Historically, the principle has been key for consensual resource extraction of the world’s deep oceans (such as fishing rights). However.........

space

+

global commons

=

false

Since the Moon Treaty of 1968,

non-spacefaring nations have repeatedly pushed for a global ratification of the principle to prevent the exploitation of the Moon’s natural resources by spacefaring nations.

Major spacefaring nations have always resisted the ratification and are currently not bounded by it.

In 2020, a new space-rhetoric has become commonplace: there is new talk of property and rights. Because LEO monopolies and Moon mining beg same question:

should a limited resource in space be equally shared between nations

or

distributed on a first-come, first-served basis?

That is what is at stake in 2020. It is the core of the conflict that I mentioned in the introduction. A conflict of interests that is potentially triggered by the decision of ITU. What should the ITU decree?
